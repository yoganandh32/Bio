Inversion of Acoustic Nonlinearity Using Immersion Ultrasound

This work presents a novel inversion-based method to measure the absolute acoustic nonlinearity parameter (β) of solids using immersion ultrasonic testing. The technique eliminates the need for tedious transducer calibration typical in contact-based approaches by employing a through-transmission setup in a water-solid-water configuration. A new theoretical model was developed to account for nonlinear wave propagation, diffraction, and attenuation across multiple layers. The model was validated with experiments on fused silica and applied to determine the β of rolled aluminum (AL1100) along different material directions. The results showed good agreement with existing literature and contact-based techniques, demonstrating the method’s potential for reliable, repeatable, and non-contact characterization of material nonlinearity.

Modeling Nonlinear Wave Diffraction in Layered Media

We developed a unified model for second harmonic wave diffraction in multilayer materials. The model uses perturbation theory and Green’s function formulation to account for nonlinear and layered effects.

Crack Detection in Railroads using Rayleigh Waves and FEM

This study simulates Rayleigh wave propagation in rail tracks using finite element methods. It demonstrates sensitivity of surface waves to small subsurface cracks for early fault detection.

Acoustic Imaging for Material Characterization of Welded Samples

We used ultrasonic C-scan and immersion testing to evaluate weld quality in metallic samples. The project aimed to identify porosity, lack of fusion, and inclusions using acoustic contrast.
